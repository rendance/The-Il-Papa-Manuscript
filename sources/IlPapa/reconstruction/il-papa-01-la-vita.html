Notes

  • Note that feet in red are explicit in the text.
  • All references to Giorgio are from Smith, Vol. II, pp. 296-298. The specific reconstruction is our own.
  • In each chorus, the reverenze are by the lady first with the man on her right, then with the man on her left. If we have confused positions at any point and notated it backwards, our apologies.
  • In measures 8-11, it literally says 3 passi, a ripresa, and three more passi - without the second ripresa we have at the end. This makes a perfectly legitimate, and quite nice, figure, if taken literally. However, it is a 3-bar figure, and Giorgio and every other figure indicate a figure length that only leaves room for 2 bars here, so we have chosen to interpret this as a parallel structure instead, where he simply omitted a word because of the parallelism. If we do assume this parallelism of structure, we get a parallelism between the sources that is compelling. On the other hand, it leaves us with a strange figure - pppr in one measure - that looks suspiciously like the passo in traverso that he uses explicitly elsewhere in this same dance. It wouldn't be the only time he describes the same thing in more than one way, seemingly just for variety, but it does seem odd.
  • The chorus has some subtle variations and distinctions in its description. Listing the descriptions out by figure from Giovannino:
    1. And the lady does a reverenza on the left foot to him who is on her right hand, and he responds with a reverenza with the right foot. Then she turns to the other and they do a reverenza with the right foot. Then all do a doppio turning to the right hand.
    2. Then they do a reverenza as above. Then all three do the doppio turning to the right hand, and in the same tempo.
    3. Then a reverenza one by one as above, and a doppio all together turning to the right hand.
    4. Then they do a reverenza and a doppio turning as above.
    5. Then they do the reverenza as above. Then the lady does a doppio turning to the right hand, and in that same tempo, the men depart with three passi and place her in their midst.
    And from Giorgio [again, from Smith, Vol. II, pps. 296-298] :
    1. The woman performs a riverentia to that man who is on the right side, and the man responds in the same tempo. She turns to that one on the left side, and performs a riverentia to him, and the man responds to her at the same tempo. All three perform a voltatonda with a doppio beginning with the right foot.
    2. The woman performs a riverentia to the man who is on the right side, and the man responds to her at the same tempo. She turns to the man who is on the left side, and performs to him a riverentia, and the man responds to her, similarly. All three perform a voltatonda with a doppio beginning with the right foot.
    3. The woman performs a riverentia to that man who is on the right side, and the man responds to her at the same tempo. She turns to that man who is on the left side of her, and performs a riverentia to him, and the man responds to her at the same tempo. All three perform a voltatonda with a doppio beginning with the right foot.
    4. The woman touches the right hand of that man who is on the right side, with a riverentia. Then she turns to the one who is on the left side, and touches the left hand with a riverentia. All three perform a voltatonda with a doppio beginning with the right foot.
    5. The woman performs a riverentia to that man who is at the right side, and the man resonds to her. Then she turns to that man who is at the left side, and performs a riverentia to him, and the man responds to her. The woman performs a voltatonda with a doppio beginning with the right foot. In that tempo, the men go to the woman with a doppio with the right foot, and they perform a mezavolta, putting the woman in the middle.

    The Giorgio versions are nearly identical, except for the obvious difference in the last figure, and the direction to touch hands in the second-to-last figure.

    Giovannino, on the other hand, seems rather inconsistent in his description. Sometimes he seems to imply they make their reverenze one at a time, sometimes together. Perhaps they are responsory, but half as long (so as to still fit the same music), perhaps the description is just poor - it's difficult to tell.

  • Note that there are three different places in Giorgio where it specifies the dancers perform a doppio portogallesi - and in each case, Giovannino indicates something different. It would be lovely if this manuscript gave us some insight into how to do this step, but that doesn't seem in the cards here.
